Disability Pride Month — Judith “Judy” Heumann

Who She Was

Judith Heumann, born in Brooklyn in 1947, contracted polio as a toddler and used a wheelchair for the rest of her life. She is widely known as the mother of the disability rights movement, a title earned across five decades of organizing, litigation, and government service that reshaped how disabled people are treated under U.S. law and around the world. She died in 2023.

Key Moments

  • Age five: Denied entry to public school, deemed a "fire hazard" because she used a wheelchair.
  • Early 1970s: Denied a New York City teaching license on medical grounds despite passing her exams. She sued the Board of Education and won, becoming one of the first wheelchair users to teach in New York State.
  • 1970: Co-founded Disabled in Action, which organized direct-action protests for disability civil rights, including shutting down traffic in Manhattan after President Nixon vetoed the Rehabilitation Act.
  • 1977: Led the Section 504 sit-in at the San Francisco Federal Building, the longest occupation of a federal building in U.S. history, forcing the government to implement the first federal disability antidiscrimination protections.
  • Later career: Served in the Clinton and Obama administrations, advised the World Bank, and worked as a senior fellow focused on international disability policy.
  • Legacy: Her memoir, Being Heumann, and the documentary Crip Camp brought her story to new generations of advocates.
"We are looking for a future of equality." — Judith Heumann
