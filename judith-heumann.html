✓Chapter 1: Brooklyn, Polio, and a Locked Door▶

Judith Heumann was born in Brooklyn in 1947 to German Jewish parents who had fled the Holocaust, having lost their own parents to it. At eighteen months old, she contracted polio and lost the use of her legs, becoming a wheelchair user for the rest of her life.

At age five, the local school turned her away, calling her a fire hazard because she used a wheelchair. She did not sit in an actual classroom until she was nine, and even then she was placed only with other disabled students, some as old as twenty one, after which classmates were routed into sheltered workshops for little or no pay. Her parents, who understood discrimination from their own history, refused to stay quiet about what was happening around her.

✓Chapter 2: A Lawsuit to Teach▶

In the early 1970s, Heumann passed both the written and oral exams for a New York City teaching license. The Board of Education failed her anyway on the medical exam, on the grounds that she could not walk and might not be able to evacuate a classroom in a fire.

She sued. The judge, Constance Baker Motley, the first Black woman to serve as a federal judge, pushed the Board to reconsider, and it backed down rather than fight the case further. Heumann became one of the first wheelchair users to teach in New York State, working for three years at the same school she had once attended as a student.

✓Chapter 3: Disabled in Action▶

In 1970, Heumann and other activists founded Disabled in Action, an organization run by and for disabled people rather than on their behalf. In 1972, after President Nixon vetoed the Rehabilitation Act, the group shut down rush hour traffic across Manhattan in protest.

The pressure worked. The Rehabilitation Act of 1973 became the first U.S. law to include federal antidiscrimination protections for disabled people. But one provision, Section 504, which barred any federally funded program from discriminating based on disability, sat unimplemented for years with no regulations behind it.

✓Chapter 4: Berkeley and a Law Still on Paper▶

Heumann enrolled at UC Berkeley's public health program, earning a master's degree in 1975 while continuing to organize. By then, Section 504 had been law for two years with no enforcement behind it, and the government showed no urgency about changing that.

Disability rights organizations across the country decided that waiting was no longer an option, and began planning a coordinated response aimed directly at the federal agency responsible for signing the regulations.

✓Chapter 5: The Section 504 Sit-In▶

On April 5, 1977, Heumann helped lead roughly 150 disabled activists into a sit-in at the Health, Education, and Welfare building in San Francisco. Sit-ins started in federal buildings in several cities that day. One by one, the others were shut down or starved out. San Francisco held.

For 26 days, the longest occupation of a federal building in U.S. history at the time, protesters slept on office floors, faced bomb threats, and kept the building running with support from outside groups, including the Black Panther Party, which delivered meals daily.

"We are looking for a future of equality."

The sit-in forced the government to finally sign the Section 504 regulations, turning a law that had existed on paper for four years into one that could actually be enforced.

✓Chapter 6: From Sit-In to Statute▶

Heumann went on to serve in the Clinton and Obama administrations, advised the World Bank on international disability policy, and worked as a senior fellow at the Ford Foundation. Section 504 became the direct blueprint for the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990.

Her memoir, Being Heumann, and the Oscar-nominated documentary Crip Camp, introduced her story to a generation that had grown up with accessible buses, ramps, and captioning already in place, most without knowing why. Heumann died in 2023, widely known as the mother of the disability rights movement.

A Note From the DEIA Advisory Group

Heumann's fight was never abstract. It was a school that would not open a door, a Board of Education that failed her on paper, a regulation signed only after 26 days of pressure. That same pattern, a right that exists on paper until someone forces enforcement, shows up constantly in the housing and disability accommodation cases ICLC handles. Her story is a reminder that access is rarely given, it is built, one refusal to leave at a time.
