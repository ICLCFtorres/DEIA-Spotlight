The History of the Word "Queer"

Presented by Yesterqueers  ·  Amanda W. Timpson, Public Historian
Video source: Yesterqueers by Amanda W. Timpson  ·  Published June 2026
Few words in the English language carry as much history, pain, and power as queer. Stretching back to the 14th century, it has served as a descriptor for the strange, a weapon turned against the vulnerable, and ultimately, a banner of radical self-determination. Explore six centuries of that journey below.
Your Progress — 0 of 6 chapters

The word queer is far older than most people realize. According to the Oxford English Dictionary — the gold standard for tracking the history of the English language — it first appears around 1390 as a verb, meaning to question or investigate.

As an adjective, meaning odd or eccentric, it arrives in the 16th century. Its earliest known written use in that form dates to around 1513, appearing in The Flyting of Dunbar and Kennedy — a Scottish poetic duel where two poets battled in verse and the audience chose the winner. Think of it as a 16th-century rap battle.

For the next nearly 400 years, queer continued in this vein: strange, excessive, aberrant, over-the-top, untrustworthy. It was a word of deviance — associated with sex workers, counterfeit money, the vague feeling of being unwell, and the state of being drunk. It described things that did not fit the expected order of things.

"For almost 400 years, queer was used to describe things that are strange or excessive, aberrant, over-the-top, untrustworthy. It's a word that is used to describe deviance." — Amanda W. Timpson

It was not until 1895 that queer was first weaponized as a slur specifically targeting gay and bisexual men — and it happened in a courtroom.

During Oscar Wilde's criminal trial for indecency, a letter written by the Marquess of Queensberry was read aloud in court. In it, Queensberry expressed his disgust at Wilde's relationship with his son, Lord Alfred Douglas, describing Wilde and his contemporaries as "snob queers."

That moment is often treated as the origin point of queer as a slur — and the start of 160-plus years of the word being used exclusively to harm, before activists "reclaimed" it at the end of the 20th century. The history is more complicated than that, and far more interesting.

Contrary to what many people believe, the Oscar Wilde trial did not launch more than a century of queer being used only as a slur. In fact, queer has functioned simultaneously as an affirming in-group identifier and as an out-group slur since at least the early 1900s.

In 1914, following social vagrant investigations in Long Beach, California, the Sacramento Bee quoted men referring to themselves and their social circles as queer — while also using the word as a derogatory descriptor. Sometimes both appeared in the same article.

In 1934, a letter discovered after a police raid on London's Caravan Club was written by a man named Cyril Kurdleon, who used the word queer to describe his own sexuality.

"Parts of our community have drawn power from identifying themselves as queer for at least a century." — Amanda W. Timpson

The reclamation was not a 1980s invention. It was already underway decades before.

By the late 1980s, activists radicalized by the AIDS crisis deliberately embraced the word queer as a unifying banner for what was then a deeply fractured community.

Using queer was both a rejection — of liberal conservatism and the Christian nationalism taking hold in government — and an embrace: a celebration of the revolutionary act of simply existing as an LGBTQ+ person in a time when doing so was genuinely dangerous.

In June 1990, an anonymous pamphlet called Queers Read This was distributed at the New York City Pride Parade. Its opening lines captured the weight of that moment:

"How can I tell you? How can I convince you, brother, sister, that your life is in danger? That every day you wake up alive, relatively happy, and a functioning human being — you are committing a rebellious act. You, as an alive and functioning queer, are revolutionary." — Queers Read This, 1990

As queer became a political identity, academics took notice. Queer theory emerged as a framework for disrupting heteronormative, binary understandings of power — broadening the lens to include the intersections of sexuality, gender, race, and class.

To queer something — used as a verb — now means to analyze or interpret it from a queer perspective: to investigate whether it can be made more accessible and relevant to people with diverse gender and sexual identities. It is not just a label. It is a method.

The word had moved from insult, to slogan, to scholarly framework — carrying its political edge at every step.

Today, queer is most often used as an affirming, inclusive umbrella term for the entire LGBTQ+ community. Historians like Amanda also use it to describe the genders and sexualities of people in the past whose lived experiences do not map cleanly onto modern categories — because, as she puts it, we have always been here and we have always been queer.

"Queer is the first word that really felt like home because it describes both my sexuality and my worldview." — Amanda W. Timpson

She is also clear that for many people — particularly Gen X and older generations — the word still carries the sting of having been used as a weapon. Both things can coexist. As Australian historian Dr. Timothy Jones observes, liberating words from non-affirming religious, clinical, or colloquial contexts and giving them new meaning is one of the defining characteristics of LGBTQ+ history.

Almost every word we use today to describe ourselves is a reclaimed slur or a repurposed clinical term. That act of reclamation — of taking back the power of language — is not new. It is one of the oldest things our community does.

ICLC Connection

Why Language Matters to Our Work

At Inner City Law Center, we represent clients who have experienced firsthand what it means to have their identity used against them — through discriminatory housing policies, biased legal decisions, and the everyday language of exclusion. The history of the word queer is, in many ways, the history of that experience.

Language shapes power. Words that were weaponized to dehumanize have been transformed — through courage, community, and persistence — into tools of solidarity and self-determination. That transformation does not happen passively. It requires people who refuse to accept the meaning others have assigned to them.

That refusal is at the heart of what our clients do every time they walk through our doors. Reclaiming identity is an act of resistance. So is showing up to fight for the people who do it.
