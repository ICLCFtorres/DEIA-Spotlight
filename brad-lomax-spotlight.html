Brad Lomax

Black Panther. Disability justice organizer. The man behind one of the most important protests you were never taught about.

Brad Lomax spent his life at the intersection of two movements that history rarely tells together, Black liberation and disability justice. His work helped force the federal government to finally enforce the country's first disability civil rights law, laying groundwork for the ADA thirteen years later. ✓Chapter 1: North Philadelphia Roots▶

Brad Lomax was born in 1950 and grew up in North Philadelphia during the height of the civil rights movement. Like many Black families in that era, the Lomaxes lived through redlining, economic segregation, and police violence, experiences that shaped Brad's sense of what needed fixing in the world.

His mother marched in the Poor People's Campaign alongside Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., and the family raised him to believe that being active and making a difference was simply expected. As a teenager he traveled to dig wells in impoverished communities, played football, and took part in school plays, an engaged, outward facing kid well before anyone thought of him as a disability rights figure.

✓Chapter 2: Howard, the Panthers, and a Diagnosis▶

In 1967, Lomax graduated high school and enrolled at Howard University, where he joined the Black Panther Party, founded the year before as a self-defense and community empowerment organization confronting systemic discrimination. His membership was significant enough that the FBI opened a file on him, which today remains one of the only written records of his life.

Around the same time, Lomax began falling frequently and was diagnosed with multiple sclerosis, a chronic disease of the central nervous system that would eventually require him to use a wheelchair. Rather than step back from the Party, he became known for something people did not associate with the Panthers, running community health efforts, including building a free health clinic in Washington, DC and staffing first aid tents at Panther events.

✓Chapter 3: Oakland and a Gap No One Else Was Filling▶

In 1973, Lomax's brother Glenn convinced him to move to Oakland, the center of Black Panther organizing. The Panthers already ran breakfast programs, free busing to prisons, and other community services, but as Lomax saw it, there was nothing built for disabled people.

Berkeley's Center for Independent Living, the first organization in the country dedicated to helping disabled people live independently rather than in institutions or with family, largely stopped at the Berkeley city line and, by most accounts, centered the concerns of white disabled men. Lomax pointed out directly that an entire population of disabled people of color was going unserved. So from 1975 to 1977, he ran his own Center for Independent Living in East Oakland, with backing from the Black Panther Party, focused on a largely Black community that had nowhere else to turn for that kind of support.

✓Chapter 4: A Law on the Books, Ignored▶

In 1973, Congress passed the Rehabilitation Act, the first major U.S. law barring discrimination against people with disabilities. But by 1977, its key enforcement provision, Section 504, still had not been implemented. On April 5, 1977, disabled activists occupied federal buildings in cities across the country to demand the government finally act.

One by one, the other sit-ins were shut down as protesters were removed or starved out. Lomax joined the sit-in at the Health, Education, and Welfare building in San Francisco and, critically, convinced the Black Panther Party to back it.

✓Chapter 5: Twenty Six Days, and the Coalition That Held▶

The San Francisco sit-in lasted 26 days, the longest occupation of a federal building in U.S. history at the time. The Black Panther Party delivered hot meals to the protesters every single day, support that participants have said was essential to the sit-in surviving as long as it did. The Panther newspaper also covered the protest when national outlets largely ignored it, bringing it the visibility it needed.

"Because you're fighting for social justice. We're going to support people who are going to fight to change the system... If the Black Panthers think that this is an important struggle, that's what we're going to do."

The sit-in finally forced the government to implement Section 504's accessibility requirements, directly paving the way for the Americans with Disabilities Act thirteen years later. It stands as one of the clearest examples in American history of a racial justice movement and a disability justice movement choosing to show up for each other, not out of symbolism, but because Lomax had spent years building that trust in person.

✓Chapter 6: Legacy▶

Lomax continued his health clinic work with the Party afterward, but by the late 1970s his MS had progressed to the point where he could no longer work, and eventually his cognitive capacity was affected as well. His family cared for him at home in his final years. Brad Lomax died on August 28, 1984.

He is remembered today as one of the founding figures of disability justice specifically because he refused to treat Blackness and disability as separate struggles. Long before intersectionality became a common framework, Lomax was living and organizing at exactly that intersection, and much of today's disability justice movement traces its lineage directly back to the model he built.

A Note From the DEIA Advisory Group

Brad Lomax's story is a reminder that the people who move a movement forward are not always the ones history remembers first. He built a Center for Independent Living because an existing one was not serving people who looked like him, and he built a coalition because he understood that racial justice and disability justice were never separate fights. That same intersection, race, poverty, and disability, shows up constantly in the housing and legal cases ICLC handles every day. His story is a useful lens for why our advocacy has to hold all of it at once.
