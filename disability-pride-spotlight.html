Disability Pride Month

Thirty five years since the ADA. A community still fighting to be seen as whole, not fixed.

Every July, disabled people around the world mark Disability Pride Month. It began quietly in the United States and has grown into a global movement built on identity, history, and a flag that was redesigned by the very community it represents. Work through the six chapters below at your own pace.

✓Chapter 1: A Month Rooted in a Law▶

Disability Pride Month traces back to July 1990, the same month the Americans with Disabilities Act was signed into law. The ADA was the first civil rights law to explicitly prohibit discrimination against disabled people in employment, public services, transportation, and public accommodations. That same summer, Boston held what is widely considered the first Disability Pride Day.

From that starting point, cities across the country and eventually around the world began holding their own Disability Pride events every July, turning a single anniversary into a recurring, global observance.

✓Chapter 2: Pride Because Of, Not In Spite Of▶

Disability Pride Month is generally organized around three goals: changing the way society thinks about and defines disability, helping disabled people unlearn internalized ableism, and promoting the idea that disability is a natural, valuable part of human diversity.

That framing matters because so much public conversation about disability still centers on limitation or inspiration rather than identity. Disability Pride pushes back on both. It does not ask anyone to pretend life is easy. It asks the world to recognize that disabled lives are not broken lives waiting to be fixed, they are full lives, and disabled people get to define what pride in that identity looks like.

✓Chapter 3: A Flag Born From Frustration▶

In 2016, writer and disability advocate Ann Magill, who has cerebral palsy, sketched out an idea for a Disability Pride flag after attending a disappointing 20th anniversary ADA event that had been tucked away out of public view. She formally released the flag in 2019.

Her original design used bright, zigzagging stripes across a black background. In her own words, the zigzag was meant to represent how disabled people constantly maneuver around barriers, having to go this way, then that way, just to move through a world that was not built with them in mind.

✓Chapter 4: The Community Redesigns Its Own Symbol▶

Shortly after the flag spread, members of the disability community raised a real concern. The bright, zigzagging pattern created a strobe-like effect on phone and computer screens, which could trigger migraines, seizures, and other sensory reactions for some disabled people themselves.

Rather than dismiss the feedback, Magill collaborated with the community to redesign the flag in 2021. The update kept the same spirit but replaced the zigzags with straight diagonal stripes and muted the colors, grouping warm and cool tones on either side of a white band to reduce flicker and help with color blindness.

Red — physical disabilities
Gold — cognitive and intellectual disabilities, neurodivergence
White — non-visible and undiagnosed disabilities
Blue — emotional and psychiatric disabilities
Green — sensory disabilities
Charcoal background — mourning those lost to ableism, abuse, and inaccessibility

Magill waived copyright on the flag entirely, placing it in the public domain so any disabled person or ally could use it freely.

✓Chapter 5: Why This Year Feels Different▶

Disability advocates have pointed out that the stakes around this year's Disability Pride Month feel higher than usual. Proposed cuts to disability benefits, tightening eligibility for programs like Medicaid, and the long tail of pandemic related chronic illness have combined to put more pressure on disabled communities at the same time as more people are joining them.

At the same time, public narratives around disability often shift toward blame or suspicion during moments of budget scarcity, questioning who "really" needs support. Disability Pride Month is one direct answer to that narrative. It is a chance to reassert that disabled lives, and the accommodations that support them, are not a burden to be minimized but a baseline of equity.

✓Chapter 6: What This Means for Our Work▶

Disability Pride Month is not an abstract observance for Inner City Law Center. Many of the clients we represent live at the intersection of disability, poverty, and housing insecurity. A tenant fighting an unlawful eviction, a client navigating a habitability claim, or someone trying to access a shelter bed may be doing all of that while also managing a physical, cognitive, or psychiatric disability that the systems around them were never designed to accommodate.

Recognizing disability pride means recognizing that accessibility is not a favor we extend to clients, it is a baseline requirement of doing this work well, from how we design our physical spaces and communications to how we advocate on someone's behalf. It also means listening when disabled staff, clients, or community members tell us something is not working, the same way the disability community listened to itself when it redesigned its own flag.

A Note From the DEIA Advisory Group

Disability Pride Month asks us to hold two things at once: real pride in a community's identity, history, and resilience, and a clear eyed look at the barriers that community still faces. Both matter to who we serve and how we serve them. We hope this spotlight gives you a starting point for both.
