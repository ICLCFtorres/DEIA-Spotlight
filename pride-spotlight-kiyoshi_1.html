The Forrest Gump of Activism
Kiyoshi Kuromiya

Born in a Japanese internment camp. Beaten on the Edmund Pettus Bridge. Co-founder of the Gay Liberation Front. One of the first people to bring free internet access to AIDS patients. This is his story.

Content sourced from Yesterqueers — a public history project by historian Amanda W. Timpson.

0 of 6 chapters opened
1
1943 – 1960
Born Behind Barbed Wire
⌄

Kiyoshi Kuromiya was born on May 9, 1943, inside the Japanese American internment camp at Heart Mountain, Wyoming — spending the first three years of his life there. He would later joke that while many activists could say they had been jailed for their beliefs, he was one of the few who could honestly say he was born a criminal.

After the war, his family returned to Monrovia, California. Kiyoshi knew from a young age that he was gay, but he didn’t have the language to describe it until he snuck into the reference section of the LA County Library at age 10 and found a copy of Kinsey’s Sexual Behavior in the Human Male.

Connection to ICLC’s work: Kiyoshi’s story begins with government-sanctioned injustice against an entire community — a reminder of why legal advocacy and civil rights protections matter.
2
1961 – 1968
Marching with Martin Luther King Jr.
⌄

Kiyoshi secured a full scholarship to the University of Pennsylvania in 1961 — but his real education happened in the streets. He met Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., Ralph Abernathy, and James Baldwin at the 1963 March on Washington, and worked closely with King until his assassination in 1968.

On March 16, 1965, he was severely beaten by Alabama state troopers during a voting rights demonstration in Montgomery. King briefly feared Kiyoshi was dead — the hospital refused to release any information on him. A gay orderly helped Kiyoshi sneak a statement to the press.

Did you know? Four months after nearly being killed in Montgomery, Kiyoshi was back in Philadelphia — participating in the first annual Reminder Day picket, which he also marked as his official coming out.
3
1968
The Dog That Never Existed
⌄

Kiyoshi was a creative strategist. To protest the use of napalm during the Vietnam War, he released an anonymous press release in April 1968 stating that a dog would be burned alive on the steps of UPenn’s Van Pelt Library.

The story exploded. The ASPCA issued an angry statement. A sitting U.S. senator officially condemned the action. Roughly 2,000 people showed up — only to find leaflets that read: “You have saved a dog. Now, how about a child or a million children?”

He was arrested by the Secret Service two days after returning from King’s funeral for distributing a protest poster, and fought the charges for three years — until the Supreme Court ruled in Cohen v. California that provocative political speech is protected by the First Amendment.

4
1969 – 1970
Co-Founding the Gay Liberation Front
⌄

In the months following the Stonewall uprising, Kiyoshi co-founded the Gay Liberation Front (GLF) with Basil O’Brien. The GLF was one of the most radical queer activist groups of its time — actively recruiting across race, class, and identity, and expressing solidarity with the Young Lords and the Black Panther Party.

In 1970, Kiyoshi represented the GLF as an openly gay delegate at the Black Panther Party convention in Philadelphia — one of the first moments of formal intersection between the Black liberation and gay liberation movements.

Intersectionality in action: Kiyoshi understood that liberation movements only succeed when they stand with each other. This was radical in 1970. It’s still foundational today.
5
1980s – 1990s
Fighting the AIDS Crisis with Information
⌄

When the AIDS epidemic began, Kiyoshi shifted his focus entirely. He co-founded ACTUP’s Philadelphia chapter and was diagnosed with AIDS himself in 1989 — likely contracting it in the early 1980s.

He taught himself to read cutting-edge research and medical literature, then translated it into plain language — founding the Critical Path Newsletter, one of the first publicly available resources for HIV information. He mailed it free of charge to thousands of subscribers worldwide, including hundreds of incarcerated individuals.

He then created one of the world’s very first websites to share HIV and AIDS information — and expanded the project to provide free internet access to AIDS patients throughout Philadelphia. He also ran a 24-hour AIDS hotline, which he staffed himself.

Technology as justice: Decades before “digital equity” entered the nonprofit lexicon, Kiyoshi was building free internet infrastructure for the most vulnerable. His work resonates directly with ICLC’s mission to remove barriers to access.
6
1999 – 2000
One Last Act of Defiance
⌄

Kiyoshi’s last act of civil disobedience came on October 6, 1999 — protesting the U.S. government’s threat to sanction countries attempting to manufacture affordable generic HIV medications.

He died on May 10, 2000 — the day after his 57th birthday — having never slowed down. His friend and fellow activist J. David Collard said Kiyoshi was one of those rare people who physiologically did not seem to need sleep.

In the margins of everything else, he also managed to serve as right-hand man to futurist Buckminster Fuller and become a nationally ranked Scrabble player.

His legacy: Kiyoshi Kuromiya embodied the belief that knowledge is power — and that power must be shared freely. He was a civil rights marcher, a queer liberationist, a technologist, and a caretaker. He never chose just one identity or one fight.
