His Story

Los Angeles

Jason Collins was born and raised right here in Los Angeles, where he and his twin brother Jarron played high school basketball alongside future actor Jason Segel. The twins were inseparable on and off the court, eventually earning spots at Stanford University.

Both brothers entered the NBA through the 2001 draft — Jarron to the Utah Jazz, Jason to the New Jersey Nets. That local upbringing, that Los Angeles foundation, was part of who he was throughout his career.

NBA Career

Collins played for six NBA teams over 13 seasons — the Nets, Grizzlies, Timberwolves, Hawks, Celtics, and Wizards. Moving around so frequently became something of an inside joke in the league, with teammates playing what they called "three degrees of Jason Collins."

Throughout all those years in the closet, he wore jersey number 98 as a silent, personal tribute to Matthew Shepard, the young gay man beaten to death in Wyoming in October 1998. No one knew why. It was a quiet act of solidarity that he carried alone for years.

Historic

The decision to come out came after Collins watched his Stanford roommate, then-Congressman Joe Kennedy, marching in a Boston Pride parade. He later wrote that he was proud of his friend but angry that he couldn't even cheer him on as a spectator without revealing himself.

In May 2013, Sports Illustrated published his essay. Those opening words — plain, direct, unflinching — made him the first active player in any of the four major U.S. men's sports leagues to publicly come out as gay. The country stopped and listened.

Courage

Coming out had a cost. Collins was a free agent when the essay ran, and the NBA season began without him. No teams called. No training camp invitations came. The silence was its own statement about how far the sports world still had to go.

In February 2014, the Brooklyn Nets signed him on a 10-day contract, eventually extending it through the end of the season. Jersey sales soared. The NBA donated proceeds to GLSEN and the Matthew Shepard Foundation. He retired from the same franchise where his career had started — 13 years, full circle.

Advocacy

After retiring, Collins became a speaker, an activist, and someone who finally let himself live openly. In May 2025 he married his longtime partner, film producer Brunson Green.

In the fall of 2025 he was diagnosed with stage 4 glioblastoma. Facing an aggressive cancer with limited treatment options, he chose to pursue experimental care — not just for himself, but as he put it, because a course of treatment he tried might one day become a new standard of care for others. Even at the end, he was thinking about the people who would come after him.

Legacy

Jason Collins died on June 7, 2026, at 47 years old. He leaves behind a husband, a family, and a generation of LGBTQ+ athletes who grew up in a world he helped make a little safer.

He was not the first gay athlete. He was the first one willing to say so out loud while still playing. That distinction — the courage it takes to be first, to stand in that exposed and uncertain place — is what ICLC's work is built on too: showing up for people when the stakes are highest, when no one else will.

Reflect
Collins wore jersey #98 for years as a private tribute — an act of solidarity no one else could see. What does it mean to honor something important when you can't yet do it publicly?
He came out knowing it might end his career. How does that kind of courage show up in the work we do at Inner City Law Center on behalf of our clients?

Inner City Law Center serves clients who are often asked to be invisible — unhoused, undocumented, marginalized. Jason Collins's story is a reminder that visibility, when someone chooses it on their own terms, can change what's possible for everyone who comes after. This Pride Month, we honor his courage and the courage of every client who trusts us with their story.
